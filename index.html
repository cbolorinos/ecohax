Use energy saving lightbulbs

Trading an incandescent bulb for a compact fluorescent one prevents 400 pounds of greenhouse gases from being emitted.

Stop drinking bottled water

When you drink bottled water you are contributing to fossil fuel emissions and creating waste that could take thousands of years to decompose. Bottled water is not better than tap water. In fact, it comes from tap water. So you might as well put your tap water in a reusable water bottle. If your area has particularly bad tap water, try installing a water filter.

Do not put your batteries in the trash

When you throw your batteries in the trash you are releasing heavy metals and toxic chemicals into our soil and water. Getting rechargable batteries reduces this waste. You can find battery recycling centers at your local Whole Foods, IKEA and Walgreens. Also, you can search for local battery dispensaries on Google or on Earth911.

Stop using paper towels

These are made of paper pulp (ultimately cut up plants) and sometimes contain bleach and/or ink, both of which can be harmful to the environment. You can either buy towels and wash them, or cut up old clothes that you don't use anymore (they will work just as well).

Stop using the air conditioner

Air conditioning uses a lot of power, most of which is likely generated by fossil fuels. The refrigerants they use also have a negative impact on the environment. There are plenty of ways to cool yourself without air conditioning. If you absolutely need to use it, turn it off when you leave the house, and consider putting it on a timer before you go to sleep.

Turn down (or off) the heater

Heaters uses a lot of power, most of which is likely generated by fossil fuels. There are plenty of ways to warm yourself without heating. If you absolutely need to use it, turn it off when you leave the house, and consider putting it on a timer before you go to sleep.

Stop using paper, plastic and Styrofoam cups

Paper cups are coated with plastic, which makes them difficult to recycle. As for Styrofoam, it is a well known contaminant to both the earth and your body. If you buy a drink and you know they serve it in Styrofoam or Paper cups, try bringing your own cup/thermos/bottle and having them serve it in that instead.

Stop using cleaning products

Cleaning products are full of toxic chemicals. They are harmful to the germs on your counter, but ultimately end up in our soil and water, where they are harmful to our wildlife, and ultimately, ourselves as well. Most household cleaning can be done with baking soda, vinegar and lemon. Stick to those. You'll help the environment, and your wallet too!

Stop using paper and plastic bags

Plastic bags harm the air, soil and water. Paper bags are less harmful, except that they encourage deforestation and are more energy intensive to transport than plastic. Canvas bags can be used for years and don't hurt the environment. You can keep one in your trunk if you use a car, or in your purse if you don't.

Take cold showers

Cold showers are not only more eco-friendly than warm ones, but also have a host of health benefits.

Turn the lights out when you leave the room

This seems impossible at first, but after a couple of days it becomes instinctual.

Cut back on meat

This seems impossible until you realize how many amazing vegetarian alternatives there are. And to think you were missing out on them because you were stuck on steak! Meat production is a huge consumer of fossil fuels and fresh water (51% of global emissions and 50% of the fresh water used in the USA). To give you an idea, adding one meat-free meal per week (for a family of four) could have the same impact as driving a hybrid car.

Buy a reusable water bottle

This makes you less likely to buy water bottles, which are harmful to the environment.

Use public transportation whenever possible

Widespread use of public transportation is the only way we can preserve our mobility without destroying our planet. Also, using it motivates your local government to invest money in improoving it. Use it whenever possible.

Reduce your air travel

Aviation is responsible for 3.5% of global emissions and is expected to rise to 5% by 2050. If it is possible to use a train, do so as they use much less energy.

Insulate your home

Proper insulation can reduce your heating bill by up to 50%. It sounds daunting but it is really not expensive or difficult to do, and the inital payment will pay for itself in a couple of months. If you want to insulate your home, check out this wikihow for useful tips.

When buying electronics, look for this logo

Electronics with the Energy Star stamp are generally 20-30% more efficient than other electronics.

Buy local foods

Produce grown in North America travels an average of 2,000 km from source to point of sale. That's a lot of fossil fuels! Also, eating local food is healthier, as the food looses a lot of it's nutritional value while being transported, and is more likely to contain preservatives. If you don't know of a farmers market near you, you can probably find one on Local Harvest.

Consider going solar

While this is expensive, the initial investment pays for itself in five years, and after 20 years you will have saved up to $64,000. Also, you will have saved approximately 100,000 pounds of C02 from being emitted into the atmosphere.
